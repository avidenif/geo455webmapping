What is this map?

Problem

Since the 1800s, Native Americans in the United States have been relocated and removed from their homes and their lands. Today, you can still see evidence of the displacement that occured. The Southwestern part of the United States is where many Indegenous people call home.

Objective

This map is designed to highlight how population, historical sites, current reservations, and boarding schools show displacement of Indegenous people and change in how land has been recognized in the Southwestern United States. Some historical sites are located outside of current reservations and in some areas population is high without reservation boundaries. This shows that less land is recognized as native than it should be. Boarding school points provide visualization of how many boarding schools were created and how many still exist today.

Audience

Individuals who may not know much about Native populations and history in the United States, specifically the Southwest.

Data Sources and Methodology

Data sources
  • Historical sites: 10 extraordinary Native American cultural sites protected on public lands
  • Reservation polygons (2022): US Domestic Sovereign Nations: Land Areas of Federally-Recognized Tribes (BIA)
  • Population Choropleth (2024): DPO5 ACS Demographic and Housing Estimates
  • Boarding Schools: NCTR-NABS Interactive Map by NAB
Data processing
  1. Download shapefiles into ArcGIS Pro (within the case of the boarding schools, I had to convert geojson to shapefile using QGIS beforehand)
  2. Simplify/clean data fields and/or polygons
  3. Project to proper projection
  4. Filter to study area if needed
  5. Add new fields and calculate fields if needed
  6. Join with other shapefiles if needed
  7. Export to GeoJSON
  8. Load into leaflet map in Pheonix Code using fetch
  9. Add/edit necessary CSS and features
  10. Push to GitHub
Web delivery methods
  1. GeoJSON (fetched in Pheonix Code)

Limitations

  • Reservation data did not include newly recognized reservations in Oklahoma.
  • Wanted to compare population to data in the 1950s, but could not find census data before 2010.
